01 — The Core Problem

Why You Can't Just Average Your MAE and MFE

You ran your NQ strategy. You collected a list of trades. Each one has a MAE (how far price went against you) and a MFE (how far price ran for you). The numbers are all over the place.

Real example from your dataset
Trade MAE (pts against you) MFE (pts in your favor) Result
Day 112 pts68 ptsWinner
Day 238 pts22 ptsLoser
Day 38 pts91 ptsWinner
Day 455 pts41 ptsLoser
Day 519 pts55 ptsWinner

The problem: if you just average the MAE, you get 26.4 pts. But what does that number mean? If you set your stop loss at 26.4 pts, you'd stop out Days 2 and 4... but what about Day 3 which only went 8 pts against you but won 91 pts? And Day 2 went 38 pts against you and was still a loser anyway.

The Real Problem

You have no single "correct" stop loss yet. Every candidate stop loss creates a completely different reality for your system. You need to test many candidates and score each one across 5 different measures, then find the best balance.

02 — The Building Blocks

MAE and MFE Explained Simply

Think of each trade as a drive from your house to the store. You're going north (your trade direction). But the road is bumpy and sometimes you drift south before getting there.

MAE
Maximum Adverse Excursion
The furthest price EVER went against you during the trade. Not where it closed. Where it went at its absolute worst point while you were still in.
MAE = entry price - lowest price hit (for longs)

Think of it as: "how much pain did I have to sit through?"

MFE
Maximum Favorable Excursion
The furthest price EVER went in your favor during the trade. Not where it closed. Where it peaked at its best point while you were in.
MFE = highest price hit - entry price (for longs)

Think of it as: "what was the best opportunity I had?"

NQ Trade on January 2, 2025 — Bearish Day

You shorted at 21,444. Here's what happened:

  • Price dipped to 21,386.5 first — bounced back to 21,404.5 — then dropped again
  • The bounce that went AGAINST your short = that was part of your MAE building up
  • Final low hit: 21,209.75 — that was your MFE peak (234 pts in your favor)
  • MAE = price went up to 21,404.5 before going down = about 37.5 pts of heat before the real move

03 — Normalization

Converting Everything to R-Multiples

Here's the key insight that solves your "multiple MAE and MFE" problem. R stands for Risk. It's your stop loss in points. Once you pick a candidate stop, every single trade's result can be expressed as a multiple of that stop.

The R-Multiple Rule

If your stop is 20 points, then 20 points = 1R. A trade that makes 60 points = +3R. A trade stopped out = -1R. Now every trade, regardless of raw point value, is on the same scale.

Same 5 trades, tested with SL = 20 pts
Trade MAE MFE MAE vs SL=20 Did it stop out? Outcome (TP=40)
Day 1 12 pts 68 pts 12 < 20 ✓ NO +2R (hit 40pt TP)
Day 2 38 pts 22 pts 38 > 20 ✗ YES -1R (stopped out)
Day 3 8 pts 91 pts 8 < 20 ✓ NO +2R (hit 40pt TP)
Day 4 55 pts 41 pts 55 > 20 ✗ YES -1R (stopped out)
Day 5 19 pts 55 pts 19 < 20 ✓ NO +2R (hit 40pt TP)

Results: [+2R, -1R, +2R, -1R, +2R] — Win rate 60%, now you can calculate all 5 metrics from this list.

This is the normalization. You're not normalizing the raw data. You're choosing a measurement lens (SL candidate), and that lens turns every trade result into a comparable R-multiple. Then you do this for every possible SL candidate.

04 — The 5 Metrics

What You're Measuring and Why Each One Matters

For every SL/TP combo you test, you compute all 5 of these. Each one tells you something different about whether that combo is actually tradeable.

EV — Expected Value
Per-Trade Profit
On average, how many R do you make per trade? This is your edge size. If EV = +0.6R and you risk $200, you average $120 profit per trade over the long run.
EV = (Win% × avg win R) - (Loss% × avg loss R)
Below 0 = losing system
0.2R+ = viable
0.5R+ = solid
PF — Profit Factor
Winners vs Losers (Gross)
Total dollars made on winners divided by total dollars lost on losers. If you made $3,000 on wins and lost $1,000 on losses, PF = 3.0. It measures how durable your edge is under pressure.
PF = sum of all winning R / sum of all losing R
Below 1.0 = losing
1.5–2.0 = decent
2.0+ = strong
RoR — Risk of Ruin
Chance of Blowing Up
The probability your account hits a point of no return before the edge pays out. A strategy can have positive EV but still blow up if the losing streaks come early and you bet too big. This catches that danger.
RoR ≈ ((1-edge) / (1+edge)) ^ (account / risk)
Above 5% = dangerous
1–5% = watch closely
Below 1% = solid
Max Streak — Consecutive Losses
Worst Losing Run
The longest back-to-back losing run in your historical simulation. This isn't bad luck — it's mathematically guaranteed to happen at some frequency. You need to know if you can stomach it before it happens live.
= longest run of -1R outcomes in sequence
3–5 = manageable
6–8 = psychologically hard
9+ = dangerous for discipline
CE — Combined Edge
Entry Quality Score
This one is different from the others. Instead of measuring your exit quality, it measures how good your ENTRY is. It asks: on trades that survived (didn't stop out), was price running harder in your direction than it was going against you?
CE = average(MFE / MAE) for surviving trades
Below 1.0 = bad entries
1.5–2.5 = decent setup
3.0+ = clean entries
Why CE is a Diagnostic Tool, Not Just Another Score

If your CE is high (say 4.0) but your EV is low (say 0.1R), it means your entries are excellent — price IS running in your direction — but your SL is too tight and you're getting stopped out of good trades before they move. Conversely, high EV but low CE means you might be catching lucky exits rather than consistently good setups. Use these two together to diagnose the real problem.

How the 5 Metrics Talk to Each Other

5-Metric Relationship Map — What Each Combo Means
Diagnosis: Lucky Exits, Weak Entries

Your EV looks good on paper but your CE is telling you that price often went against you just as much as it went for you. This happens when your TP is set too close and you're banking small wins before the trade can prove itself. Your entries might not be as clean as you think.

Diagnosis: SL Too Tight

Your entries are excellent — when trades survive, MFE runs way harder than MAE. But your SL is killing you. You're being stopped out of winners that would have been 3R moves if you gave them a few more points of room. Fix: widen the SL to the next candidate tier.

Diagnosis: Position Size is Too Big

Good ratio of winners to losers (PF high) but RoR is high means you're betting too large relative to your account. The strategy is fine — the sizing is reckless. Lower your risk per trade from 2% to 1% and watch RoR drop dramatically.

This Is What You're Looking For

EV is positive, PF is above 2.0, RoR is below 1%, Max Streak is under 7, and CE is above 2.0. This means the SL/TP combo is not just profitable — it's stable, your entries are clean, and you can actually survive the drawdowns psychologically. This is a Pareto-dominant cell.

05 — Mickey's Bell Curve

The Bell Curve: Where to Get Your SL Candidates From

This is what went over your head. Here's the full breakdown.

Most traders just test SL = 15, 20, 25, 30 pts because those feel like round numbers. Mickey's point is: use the actual shape of your MAE data to decide which stops are worth testing.

Step 1 — What Is a Bell Curve?

If you took all your trades and plotted how far price went against each one (MAE), you'd see a shape emerge. Most trades have a "typical" amount of heat. Very few have almost zero heat, and very few have extreme heat. Most cluster in the middle. That cluster shape is the bell curve.

The Two Numbers That Define the Bell

Mean (average MAE, called "mu") — the center of the bell. The most common amount of heat your trades experience. If your average MAE is 18 pts, the bell peaks at 18.

Std Dev (spread, called "sigma") — how wide the bell is. If sigma = 8, it means most of your trades are between 10 and 26 pts of heat (within 1 standard deviation of 18).

Step 2 — The Magic Rule: 68 / 95 / 99.7

Once you know the bell curve of your MAE, these percentages tell you exactly how many of your trades stay UNDER any given stop level:

Stop Level % of Trades Covered % Stopped Out Plain English
Mean (18 pts) 50% 50% Half your trades get stopped. Terrible stop placement.
+1 Sigma (26 pts) 84% 16% Only 1 in 6 trades gets stopped. Getting better.
+1.5 Sigma (30 pts) 93% 7% Only 1 in 14 stopped. Strong candidate.
+2 Sigma (34 pts) 97.5% 2.5% Almost nothing stopped. But TP also needs to be wider.
The Fat Tail Warning (The Catch)

Real NQ data is NOT a perfect bell curve. On extreme days, price can go way further against you than the bell curve would predict. The bell says a 3-sigma event is 0.3% likely — but in NQ, those "impossible" moves happen every few months. This means: always add a 10-15% buffer on top of your bell curve stop candidates. The bell gives you the floor, not the ceiling.

Step 3 — Do the Same for MFE to Get TP Candidates

Run the exact same bell curve analysis on your MFE data. The mean MFE tells you the average profit potential. Use the same percentile tiers (50th, 65th, 75th, 85th) to generate TP candidates that are grounded in what your trades actually DO, not what you hope they will do.

  1. Generate SL and TP Candidates from Bell Curve

    Take all your MAE values. Compute the mean and standard deviation. Use mean, mean+0.5s, mean+1s, mean+1.5s, mean+2s as your SL grid. Do the same with MFE for TP candidates. You now have a meaningful grid — not arbitrary round numbers — grounded in what your actual trades experienced. Add a 10–15% buffer per level to account for fat tail days.

  2. Simulate Every SL/TP Combo Against Every Trade

    For each combination of [SL, TP]: go through every trade. If the trade's MAE exceeds SL, outcome = -1R. If the trade's MFE reaches TP while MAE stayed under SL, outcome = +(TP/SL)R. Record the full list of R outcomes for that combo.

  3. Compute All 5 Metrics per Grid Cell

    From the R outcome list: compute EV (mean of outcomes), PF (sum of positives / abs sum of negatives), RoR (Kelly-based formula using win rate and R ratio), Max Streak (longest consecutive negative run), and CE (average MFE/MAE across surviving trades only). Every grid cell now has 5 numbers attached to it.

  4. Run the Pareto Front and Find the Knee

    Feed all 5 metrics per cell into the Pareto optimizer. Maximize EV, PF, CE. Minimize RoR, Max Streak. Cells on the Pareto front are those where no other cell beats them on ALL 5 simultaneously. From those dominant cells, apply your composite score to find the "knee" — the single best balanced trade-off.

07 — Pareto Front

Why There's No Single "Best" Combo — and How Pareto Finds It Anyway

Here's the core tension that makes this hard. Tighter stop = higher loss rate but smaller losses. Wider stop = lower loss rate but bigger losses when they happen. Every metric responds differently to this tradeoff.

What "Pareto Dominant" Means in Plain English

A cell is Pareto dominant if you cannot find any other cell that is better (or equal) on ALL 5 metrics at the same time. In other words: there's no "free lunch" alternative. If you want more EV, you'll have to accept more RoR or a longer losing streak. The Pareto front maps all the cells where you've already used up every free improvement — every remaining choice involves a real trade-off.

08 — The Verdict

The One-Sentence Answer to Your Question

Your raw MAE and MFE values with different point sizes are NOT the problem — they're the data. The normalization happens automatically the moment you pick a candidate stop: that stop becomes "1R" and every trade result converts into a multiple of it. The bell curve tells you which stop candidates are worth testing (not arbitrary round numbers). The 5-metric grid scores every candidate. The Pareto front selects the ones you cannot improve without giving something up. Your CE metric sits alongside EV, PF, RoR, and streak to make sure you're not just optimizing for one thing and breaking something else.

Quick Reference — What to Look For in Your Best Combo

Metric Minimum Threshold What Happens If You Ignore It
EV (R) Above +0.3R You're trading a system with no edge
Profit Factor Above 1.8 Losers overwhelm winners in gross terms
Risk of Ruin Below 2% Account blows before the edge pays out
Max Streak Below 8 Psychologically impossible to stay disciplined

The Final Rule

The best SL/TP combo is not the one with the highest EV. It is the combo that satisfies ALL 5 thresholds simultaneously AND sits on the Pareto front. That is the only mathematically defensible answer. Everything else is guessing with extra steps.
