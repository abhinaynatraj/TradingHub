Quarter-of-the-Hour Study

What this measures
Each NQ hourly candle split into four 15-minute quarters.
We slice every hour into Q1 (:00–:14), Q2 (:15–:29), Q3 (:30–:44), and Q4 (:45–:59), then ask six separate questions about how price behaves inside the hour. Each tab below answers one of them.

Which quarter holds the hour's high — and which holds the low?
For every hour in the dataset, we record which quarter contained the highest high and which contained the lowest low. A flat 25/25/25/25 distribution would mean extremes are random in time. A skew tells you when to expect the print.
▲ Quarter that contains the hour's HIGH
▼ Quarter that contains the hour's LOW
The takeaway is symmetric: Q1 and Q4 are the most common locations for both highs and lows. Hours tend to make their first big move early (Q1) and then either drift, or print a fresh extreme right at the close (Q4). The middle quarters are quieter — a Q2 or Q3 high/low is roughly half as common as a Q1/Q4 one.
Within the hour, does the high or the low form first?
Comparing the bar that printed the hour's high against the bar that printed its low — whichever came first wins. "Tie" means both printed in the same minute.
The split is roughly 50/50 on the dataset overall, but small persistent biases at certain hours can be a useful tell — if a particular hour reliably prints its low first, you have a directional anchor for the range. Use the slice toggles below to look for those edges.
Is each quarter directionally biased — and how big are its candles?
For each of Q1–Q4 we compute the share of up-closes vs down-closes, the average and median range, and the body-to-range ratio (a measure of conviction — high = trending, low = wicky).

Two stable patterns:
  • Q1 and Q3 are the largest-range quarters on average — these are the "action" windows.
  • The body-to-range ratio sits near 50% in every quarter, meaning roughly half the range is wick.
A meaningful directional bias would show up as one quarter's up-close rate drifting noticeably above or below 50%. The aggregate shows it's balanced — but slicing by hour can surface real edges.
Does Q1's direction predict the rest of the hour?
Conditional probabilities: given Q1 closed up (or down), what's the chance the full hour also closes up? And what's the chance Q4 reverses Q1?
The signal here is real: when Q1 closes up, the full hour closes up about 70% of the time (and symmetrically for down). Q1 is a strong directional anchor for the hour — but that doesn't mean subsequent quarters trend in the same direction. Q2/Q3/Q4 individually run roughly 50/50 regardless of Q1. The hour's net direction comes from magnitude set in Q1, not continued momentum.
When Q1 sets an extreme, how often does it survive the hour?
"Hold rate" = the fraction of hours where the Q1 high (or low) ends up being the hour's high (or low) — i.e. no later quarter exceeded it. "Overshoot" = when it fails, by how much does the next quarter break through?
Q1 extremes hold around 36–40% of the time — meaning nearly two-thirds of hours print a fresh extreme later. When the Q1 high does get broken, the median overshoot is small (~8 NQ points), so the level is still useful as a near-term reference even if it doesn't survive.
Does a wide Q1 predict a wide hour?
We bucket hours by Q1 range into quintiles (smallest 20% → largest 20%) and look at the average full-hour range and the additional "remaining range" after Q1.
Strong positive scaling: large Q1 ranges predict large hours. The top Q1-range quintile averages ~99 NQ points of full-hour range vs ~11 points for the smallest. A loud open keeps making noise — useful for sizing expectations of where the hour might travel. Notably, Q1 hold rates also rise with Q1 range: a wide opening quarter is less likely to be exceeded.
