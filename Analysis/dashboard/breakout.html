Breakout Follow-Through

What this measures
When an hour breaks out, does the next hour push further?
A bullish breakout is an hour that closes above the prior hour's high. A bearish breakout is an hour that closes below the prior hour's low. We then ask: did the next hour take out the breakout bar's extreme — or did it reverse? Filter the dataset below to slice by year, hour-of-day, day-of-week, or direction.

How to read this page. A breakout is a directional setup — the question is whether to fade it or follow it. The follow-through rate tells you how often pressing in the breakout's direction would have worked. The reversal rate tells you how often it failed badly enough to take out the opposite side. A heavy Q1 bias means momentum doesn't wait — if the next hour is going to confirm, it usually does so in the first 15 minutes. A flat distribution across Q1–Q4 would suggest the move drifts in slowly. Most breakouts skew strongly toward Q1.
Does where the next hour opens change the odds?
We split each next-hour by where it opens relative to the prior hour's mid-price (the average of its high and low). Opening above prev-mid suggests follow-through is "pre-confirmed" by the gap; opening below suggests indecision.
A consistent edge here means the open of H+1 is itself a tradeable signal — if the new hour opens on the breakout side of prev-mid, you can lean in earlier than waiting for follow-through to print.
